Customer service at large telecommunications companies had traditionally been organized around websites and call centres. Customers needing to reset a PIN, manage their package, schedule an engineer visit, or troubleshoot an issue often had no choice but to navigate support websites or wait on hold to speak with an agent.

As smartphones had become an increasingly important part of everyday life, this model was beginning to feel outdated.

Sky is one of the UK's largest media and telecommunications companies, providing television, broadband, and phone services to millions of households. As part of its Digital First strategy, the company saw an opportunity to rethink how customers managed their accounts and resolved service issues beyond traditional support channels.

I led the design of Sky Service, a mobile-first customer service experience that allowed customers to manage their account, troubleshoot common issues, schedule appointments, and access support directly from their phones. Rather than simply recreating existing website functionality within an app, the project explored how mobile could create a more contextual and responsive service experience.

<Media src="/case-03-service-app-ia.png" alt="Information architecture diagram showing Sky Service organised around customer problems rather than separate support channels, account tools, and content types" caption="Early information architecture explorations helped us rethink customer support as a connected service experience rather than a collection of separate tools and channels." />

Customer journeys consistently span support content, account tools, and service channels. Customers thought in terms of problems they wanted to solve, not the organizational boundaries between different parts of the experience.

<PullQuote>
Customers could move seamlessly between understanding a problem and taking the action needed to resolve it.
</PullQuote>

A customer trying to upgrade a package, activate a service, reset a PIN, or troubleshoot an issue did not care whether the solution lived in a help article, an account-management flow, or a call-centre process. They simply wanted to resolve the issue as quickly as possible.

This led to a model we internally called "next best actions." Rather than treating account management, help content, and support tools as separate destinations, the experience connected them into a single network of contextual journeys.

<Media src="/case-03-service-next-best.png" alt="Screen designs showing the next best actions model, where help content and account management tools are connected into contextual customer journeys" caption="Next best actions connected guidance and account tools, helping customers move directly from understanding a problem to resolving it." />

Customers could move seamlessly between understanding a problem and taking the action needed to resolve it. Help content linked directly to relevant account actions, while account-management experiences surfaced guidance and next steps when customers needed them most.

Designing for mobile also created opportunities that did not exist on the web. Rather than asking customers to remember a problem until they were back at a computer, support became available wherever they were. Account management, troubleshooting, and service actions could happen in the moment, turning the phone into the primary touchpoint for customer service.

But self-service was not the goal in itself. When customers could not resolve an issue on their own, the app provided a direct path to human support. Customers could see whether the call centre was open, view their place in the queue, request a callback, and connect with an agent without leaving the experience. Rather than treating digital and human support as separate channels, the app brought them together into a single service journey.

<InlineImage src="/case-04-service-app-store-rank.jpg" alt="App Store top charts showing Sky Service at number 1" side="right" columns={3} mobileColumns={2} caption="Sky Service ranked #1 in the UK App Store after its launch" />By connecting account management tools, support content, and contextual guidance into a single experience, we helped customers move seamlessly from understanding a problem to resolving it while reducing their dependence on traditional customer service channels.

The app launched after approximately eight months of development, reached number one on the UK App Store, and received an Appy Award for Best Productivity App. More importantly, it established a new model for digital customer service that connected support and action into a single experience.
